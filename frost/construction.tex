\section{Distributed Key Generation and FROST Signatures}

We present this section in two parts.
First, we give a distributed key generation (DKG) construction,
that is a variant of Pedersen DKG~\cite{Pedersen91},
with zero-knowledge proofs of knowledge of each participant's contribution.
We refer to this DKG as PedPop,
following convention of subsequent work by this thesis author~\cite{BellareCKMTZ22}.
In the second part,
we present the FROST signature scheme,
which is a threshold Schnorr signature,
outputting a signature that is valid under the Schnorr signature verification
algorithm~\cite{Schnorr91}.

\subsection{PedPop: Key Generation for FROST}

We next present PedPop,
a simplified two-round DKG that builds on Schnorr signatures and Pedersen's VSS.
In later work~\cite{BellareCKMTZ22},
this DKG was given the name PedPop,
which will also be used here.
%
We show PedPop in Figure~\ref{fg:pedpop}.
PedPop builds upon the DKG by Pedersen~\cite{Pedersen91},
with a proof of knowledge to protect against against rogue key attacks.

Each participant performs the role of a dealer,
by generating secret shares and a commitment via Pedersen's VSS
with respect to a randomly sampled secret.
Then,
each participant sends the shares to their respective participant (saving one
share for itself),
and sends the commitment to all participants.
Additionally,
each participant sends a proof of knowledge (specifically, a
Schnorr signature) that demonstrates knowledge of the secret that is shared. In
doing so, PedPop  protects against rogue key attacks~\cite{RistenpartY07}.
Without this proof of knowledge,
a rushing adversary (i.e., one that can speak last) could perform a rogue key
attack by picking  their public key with respect to any other participant's public key,
effectively cancelling out key material or forcing the group public key to hit some target
value.

\paragraph{Protocol Description.}
To generate long-lived key shares,
PedPop builds upon Pedersen's DKG for key generation; we detail
these protocol steps in
Figure~\ref{fg:pedpop}.
Note that Pedersen's DKG is
simply where each participant
executes Feldman's VSS as the dealer in parallel, and derives their
secret share as the sum of the shares received from each of the $n$
VSS executions. In addition to the
base Pedersen DKG protocol, FROST additionally requires each
participant to demonstrate knowledge of their secret by providing
other participants with proof in zero knowledge, instantiated as a Schnorr
signature, to protect against rogue-key attacks~\cite{BellareBS03}.

To begin the key generation protocol, a set of participants must be formed
using some out-of-band mechanism decided upon by the implementation.
After participating in PedPop, each participant $P_i$
holds a value
$(i, \sk_i)$ that is their long-lived secret signing share.
Participant $P_i$'s public key share $\pk_i = g^{\sk_i}$ is
used by other participants to verify the correctness of $P_i$'s
signature shares in the following signing phase, while
the group public key $\pk$ can be used by parties external to the group
to verify signatures issued by the group in the future.

\textbf{View of Commitment Values.} As required for \emph{any} multi-party
protocol using Feldman's VSS,
the key generation stage in PedPop
similarly requires participants to maintain a consistent view of commitments
$\Vec{C_i}, 1 \leq i \le n$
issued during the execution of PedPop. In this work, we
assume participants broadcast the
commitment values honestly (e.g., participants do not provide different
commitment values to a subset of participants).

\textbf{Security tradeoffs.} While Gennaro et al.~\cite{GennaroJKR07}
describe a ``Stop, Kill, and Rewind'' attack against Pedersen DKG (where the protocol
terminates and is re-run if misbehaviour is detected) as vulnerable to
influence by the adversary, we note that in a real-world setting,
good security practices typically require that the cause of misbehaviour is
investigated once it has been detected; the protocol is not allowed to
terminate and re-run continuously until
the adversary finds a desirable output.
Further, many protocols in practice do not prevent an adversary from
aborting and re-executing key agreement at any point in the protocol;
adversaries in protocols such as the widely used TLS protocol can skew the
distribution of the resulting key simply by re-running the protocol.

However, implementations wishing for a robust DKG can simply use the DKG by Gennaro et al.~\cite{GennaroJKR07} to generate keys for the FROST signing protocol instead.
Note that the efficiency of the DKG for the key generation
phase is not extremely critical,
because this operation
must be done only once per key generation for long-lived keys.
For the per-signature operations, FROST optimizes the generation of random
values \emph{without} utilizing a DKG, as discussed next.

We now introduce the signing protocol for FROST. This operation builds upon
known techniques in the literature~\cite{AbidinAM20,SchroderU17} by employing additive secret sharing and share
conversion to non-interactively generate the nonce value for each
signature.
However, signing operations in FROST additionally leverage a novel binding
technique to avoid known forgery attacks without limiting concurrency.
Verification of signatures is identical to (single-party) Schnorr signatures.

We present FROST signing in two parts: a pre-processing phase and a
single-round signing phase. However, these stages can be combined into a two-round protocol if desired.

At a high level,
the ROS-style attack of Drijvers et al.~\cite{DrijversEFKLNS19} requires the adversary to either
see the victim's commitment values in the first round before selecting their own commitment,
or to adaptively choose the message to
be signed, so that the adversary can manipulate the resulting challenge
for the set of participants performing a group signing operation.
To prevent this attack without limiting concurrency,
FROST ``binds'' each participant's response to a specific
message as well as the set of participants and their commitments used for that
particular signing operation. In doing so, combining responses
over different messages or participant/commitment pairs results in an invalid
signature, thwarting ROS-style attacks such as those of Drijvers et al.

\paragraph{Coordinator Role.}
FROST assumes an external mechanism to choose the set
$\coalition \subseteq \{1, \ldots, n \}$ of signers, where $t \leq \abs{\coalition} \leq n$.
The coordinator is trusted to not perform denial of service attacks,
but otherwise is untrusted.
Even if the coordinator were untrusted,
FROST remains unforgeable.
The coordinator can be a stand-alone entity,
or may also be a signer as well.


\paragraph{Preprocessing Stage.}
As a reminder, the public parameters $\pp$ are implicitly given as input to all algorithms and protocols.
The coalition $\coalition$ is required to be ordered to ensure consistency.

In this setting, $\ell$ determines the number of
nonces that are generated and their corresponding commitments that are
published in a single preprocessing step.
Implementations that do not wish to cache commitments can instead
use a two-round signing protocol, where participants publish a single
commitment in the first round.

Each participant $P_i$ begins by generating a list of
\emph{single-use} private nonce pairs and corresponding public commitment shares
$\langle((d_{ij}, D_{ij} = g^{d_{ij}}), (e_{ij}, E_{ij} = g^{e_{ij}})) \rangle_{j=1}^\ell$,
where
$j$ is a counter that identifies the next nonce/commitment share pair available
to use for signing.
Each $P_i$ then publishes $(i, L_i)$, where $L_i$ is their list of
commitment shares
$L_i  = \langle(D_{ij}, E_{ij}) \rangle_{j=1}^\ell$.
The set of $(i, L_i)$ tuples are then stored by any entity that might perform
the coordinator role during signing.

\paragraph{Online Signing Protocol.}
At the beginning of the signing protocol in Figure~\ref{fig:frost},
the coordinator selects  a coalition of parties $\coalition \subseteq \set{n}, \lvert \coalition \rvert \geq t$ to perform signing.
Let $\coalition$ be the set of those $\alpha$ participants.
The coordinator then selects the next available commitment
$(D_i, E_i) : i \in \coalition$,
which are later used to generate a secret share to a random commitment $R$ for
the signing group.\footnote{Each participant
contributes to the group commitment $R$, which corresponds to the
commitment $g^k$ to the nonce $k$
in step 1 of the single-party Schnorr signature scheme in
Section~\ref{background:schnorr}.}
%
The coordinator then creates the set $B$, where
$B$ is the ordered list of tuples $\langle(i, D_i, E_i)\rangle_{i \in S}$.
The coordinator then sends $(m, B)$ to every $P_i, i \in \coalition$.

After receiving $(m, B)$ from the coordinator to initialize a signing operation,
each participant checks that $m$ is a message they are willing to sign.
Then, using $m$ and $B$,
all participants derive the ``binding values'' $\rho_i, i \in C$ such that
$\rho_i = \HashNon(i, m, B)$, where $\HashNon$ is a hash function whose outputs are in $\mathbb{Z}_q^*$.

Each participant then
computes the commitment $R_i$ for each participant in $\coalition$ by
deriving $R_i = D_i \cdot (E_i)^{\rho_i}$.
Doing so binds the
message, the set of signing participants, and each participant's commitment
to each signature share.
This binding technique thwarts the attack of Drijvers et al.\cite{DrijversEFKLNS19},
as attackers cannot combine signature shares across disjoint signing operations or permute the set of signers or published
commitments for each signer.

The commitment for the set of signers
is then simply $R = \prod_{i \in \coalition} R_i$.
As in single-party Schnorr signatures, each participant computes the challenge
$c = \HashSig(R, \pk, m)$.

The resulting secret nonce is
$k = \sum_{i \in \coalition} k_i$, where each $k_i = d_i + e_i \cdot \rho_i$,
and $(d_i, e_i)$ correspond to the $(D_i = g^{d_i},
E_i=g^{e_i})$ values published during the Preprocess stage.
%Recall that if the $k_i$ are
%\emph{additive} shares of $k$, then the $\frac{k_i}{\lambda_i}$ are
%\emph{Shamir} shares of $k$, such that each $\lambda_i$ is the Lagrange
%coefficient for the $i\supth$ participant over the set $S$.

Each participant's response $z_i$ to the challenge
can be computed using the single-use nonces $(d_i, e_i)$ and the
long-term secret shares $\sk_i$, converted to additive form:
\[
  z_i = d_i + (e_i \cdot \rho_i) + \lambda_i \cdot \sk_i \cdot c
\]

The coordinator finally checks the consistency of each participant's reported
$z_i$ with their commitment share $(D_i, E_i)$ and their public key share
$\pk_i$.
If every participant issued a correct $z_i$, the group's response is
$z = \sum_{i \in \coalition} z_i$, and the group signature on $m$ is $\sigma=(R, z)$.
This signature is
verifiable to anyone performing a standard Schnorr verification operation
with $\pk$ as the public key (Section~\ref{background:schnorr}).



\paragraph{Handling Ephemeral Outstanding Shares.} Because each nonce and
commitment share generated during the first round of signing described in Figure~\ref{fig:frost}
must be used \emph{at most once},
participants should delete these values after using them in a signing operation,.
An accidentally reused $(d_{ij}, e_{ij})$ can lead to exposure of the participant's
long-term secret $\sk_i$.

However, if the coordinator chooses to re-use a commitment set $(D_i,
E_i)$ during the signing protocol,  doing so simply results in
the participant $P_i$ aborting the protocol, and consequently does not increase the power of the coordinator.

\subsection{Correctness of FROST}
\label{frost:correctness}

Signatures in FROST
are constructed from two polynomials;
the first polynomial $F_1(x)$ defines the secret sharing of the private
signing key $\sk$ (such that $\pk = g^{\sk}$) and the
second polynomial $F_2(x)$ defines the secret sharing of the nonce $k$
such that
\(
k = \sum_{i \in \coalition} d_i + e_i \cdot \rho_i
\)
using the associated public data $(m, B)$ to determine $\rho_i$.
During the key generation phase,
the first polynomial $F_1(x) = \sum_{j=1}^n f_j(x)$ is generated
such that the secret key shares are $\sk_i = F_1(i)$ and the secret key is $\sk = F_1(0)$.

During the signature phase,
each of the
participants in the coalition $\coalition$ selected for signing use a pair of nonces $(d_i, e_i)$ to
define a degree $\lvert\coalition-1 \rvert$ polynomial $F_2(x)$, interpolating the
values $(i,\frac{d_i + e_i \cdot \HashNon(i, m, B)}{\lambda_i})$,
such that $F_2(0) = \sum_{i \in \coalition} d_i + e_i \cdot \rho_i$.

Then let $F_3(x) = F_2(x) + c \cdot F_1(x)$, where
$c = \HashSig(R, \pk, m)$.  Now $z_i$
equals $d_i + (e_i \cdot \rho_i) + \lambda_i \cdot \sk_i \cdot c =
\lambda_i (F_2(i) + c \cdot F_1(i)) = \lambda_i F_3(i)$, so $z = \sum_{i \in \coalition} z_i$
is simply the Lagrange interpolation of $F_3(0) = (\sum_{i \in \coalition} d_i + e_{ij} \cdot \rho_i) + c
\cdot s$.  Because $R = g^{\sum_{i \in \coalition} d_i + e_i \cdot \rho_i}$ and $c
= \HashSig(R, \pk, m)$, $(R, z)$ is a
correct Schnorr signature on $m$.
