

\section{FROST Signatures}

We next present the FROST signature scheme,
which is a threshold Schnorr signature,
outputting a signature that is valid under the Schnorr signature verification
algorithm~\cite{Schnorr91};
see Section~\ref{background:schnorr}.


\subsection{Key Generation}

The FROST threshold signature scheme assumes that all parties are given Shamir secret shares of a signing key $\sk \in \Zq$,
such that the public key is $\pk = g^{\sk}$.
In other words,
we assume each participant $i \in \set{n}$ receives the secret signing key
share $\sk_i$,
such that $\{ (i, \sk_i) \}_{i=1}^n \rgets \shamir.\IssueShares(\sk, n, \thresh)$.
Key generation can be performed via a trusted dealer,
or in a distributed manner via a distributed key generation (DKG) protocol.

We now introduce the signing protocol for FROST. This operation builds upon
known techniques in the literature~\cite{AbidinAM20,SchroderU17} by employing additive secret sharing and share
conversion to non-interactively generate the nonce value for each
signature.
However, signing operations in FROST additionally leverage a novel binding
technique to avoid known forgery attacks without limiting concurrency.
Verification of signatures is identical to (single-party) Schnorr signatures.
As discussed in Remark~\ref{rem:defer}
in Section~\ref{threshold-sig-background},
giving the coalition $\coalition$ and message $\msg$ can be safely deferred to Round~2.

We present FROST signing in two parts: a pre-processing phase and a
single-round signing phase. However, these stages can be combined into a two-round protocol if desired.

At a high level,
the ROS-style attack of Drijvers et al.~\cite{DrijversEFKLNS19} requires the adversary to either
see the victim's commitment values in the first round before selecting their own commitment,
or to adaptively choose the message to
be signed, so that the adversary can manipulate the resulting challenge
for the set of participants performing a group signing operation.
To prevent this attack without limiting concurrency,
FROST ``binds'' each participant's response to a specific
message as well as the set of participants and their commitments used for that
particular signing operation. In doing so, combining responses
over different messages or participant/commitment pairs results in an invalid
signature, thwarting ROS-style attacks such as those of Drijvers et al.

\paragraph{Coordinator Role.}
Our presentation of FROST assumes a coordinator role to choose the set
$\coalition \subseteq \{1, \ldots, n \}$ of signers, where $t \leq \abs{\coalition} \leq n$.
The coordinator is trusted to not perform denial of service attacks,
but otherwise is untrusted.
Even if the coordinator were fully malicious,
FROST remains unforgeable.
The coordinator can be a stand-alone entity,
or may also be a signer as well.


\paragraph{First Signing Round $\Sign_1$.}
As a reminder, the public parameters $\pp$ are implicitly given as input to all algorithms and protocols.
The coalition $\coalition$ is required to be ordered to ensure consistency.

In this setting, $\ell$ determines the number of
nonces that are generated and their corresponding commitments that are
published in a single preprocessing step.
Implementations that do not wish to cache commitments can instead
use a two-round signing protocol, where participants publish a single
commitment in the first round.

Each participant $i \in \coalition$ begins by generating a list of
\emph{single-use} private nonce pairs and corresponding public commitment shares
$((d_{ij}, D_{ij} = g^{d_{ij}}), (e_{ij}, E_{ij} = g^{e_{ij}}))_{j=1}^\ell$,
where
$j$ is a counter that identifies the next nonce/commitment share pair available
to use for signing.
Each participant $i \in \coalition$ then publishes its set of commitments
$(i, D_{ij}, E_{ij} )_{j=1}^\ell$.
These sets are then stored by any entity that might perform
the coordinator role during signing.

This first signing round can be performed in a batched manner,
as an optional pre-processing stage,
as this round is performed both independent of the message to be signed.

\paragraph{Second Signing Round $\Sign_2$.}
At the beginning of the signing protocol in Figure~\ref{fig:frost},
the coordinator selects  a coalition of parties $\coalition \subseteq \set{n}, \lvert \coalition \rvert \geq t$ to perform signing.
The coordinator then selects the next available commitment
$(i, D_i, E_i) : i \in \coalition$,
which are later used to generate a secret share to a random commitment $R$ for
the signing group.\footnote{Each participant
contributes to the group commitment $R$, which corresponds to the
commitment $g^k$ to the nonce $k$
in step 1 of the single-party Schnorr signature scheme in
Section~\ref{background:schnorr}.}
%
The coordinator then creates the set $B$, where
$B$ is the ordered list of tuples $\langle(i, D_i, E_i)\rangle_{i \in \coalition}$.
The coordinator then sends $(m, B)$ to every participant $i \in \coalition$.

After receiving $(m, B)$ from the coordinator to initialize a signing operation,
each participant checks that $m$ is a message they are willing to sign.
Then, using $m$ and $B$,
all participants derive the ``binding values'' $\bindingf_i, i \in \coalition$ such that
$\bindingf_i = \HashNon(i, \pk, m, B)$, where $\HashNon$ is a hash function whose outputs are in $\mathbb{Z}_q^*$.

Each participant then
computes the commitment $R_i$ for each participant in $\coalition$ by
deriving $R_i = D_i \cdot (E_i)^{\bindingf_i}$.
Doing so binds the
message, the set of signing participants, and each participant's commitment
to each signature share.
This binding technique thwarts the attack of Drijvers et al.\cite{DrijversEFKLNS19},
as attackers cannot combine signature shares across disjoint signing operations or permute the set of signers or published
commitments for each signer.

The commitment for the set of signers
is then simply $R = \prod_{i \in \coalition} R_i$.
As in single-party Schnorr signatures, each participant computes the challenge
$c = \HashSig(R, \pk, m)$.

The resulting secret nonce is
$k = \sum_{i \in \coalition} k_i$, where each $k_i = d_i + e_i \cdot \bindingf_i$,
and $(d_i, e_i)$ correspond to the $(D_i = g^{d_i},
E_i=g^{e_i})$ values published during the preprocessing stage.
%Recall that if the $k_i$ are
%\emph{additive} shares of $k$, then the $\frac{k_i}{\lambda_i}$ are
%\emph{Shamir} shares of $k$, such that each $\lambda_i$ is the Lagrange
%coefficient for the $i\supth$ participant over the set $S$.

Each participant's response $z_i$ to the challenge
can be computed using the single-use nonces $(d_i, e_i)$ and the
long-term secret shares $\sk_i$, converted to additive form:
\[
  z_i = d_i + (e_i \cdot \bindingf_i) + \lambda_i \cdot \sk_i \cdot c
\]

\paragraph{Combine.}
The coordinator (optionally) checks the consistency of each participant's reported
$z_i$ with their commitment share $(D_i, E_i)$ and their public key share
$\pk_i$.
If every participant issued a correct $z_i$,
the group's response is $z = \sum_{i \in \coalition} z_i$,
and the group signature on $m$ is $\sigma=(R, z)$.
This signature is verifiable to anyone performing a standard Schnorr verification operation
with $\pk$ as the public key (Section~\ref{background:schnorr}).



\paragraph{Handling Ephemeral Outstanding Shares.} Because each nonce and
commitment share generated during the first round of signing described in Figure~\ref{fig:frost}
must be used \emph{at most once},
participants should delete these values after using them in a signing operation,.
An accidentally reused $(d_{ij}, e_{ij})$ can lead to exposure of the participant's
long-term secret $\sk_i$.

However, if the coordinator chooses to re-use a commitment set $(D_i,
E_i)$ during the signing protocol,  doing so simply results in
the participant $P_i$ aborting the protocol, and consequently does not increase the power of the coordinator.

\subsection{Correctness of FROST}
\label{frost:correctness}

Signatures in FROST
are constructed from two polynomials;
the first polynomial $F_1(x)$ defines the secret sharing of the private
signing key $\sk$ (such that $\pk = g^{\sk}$) and the
second polynomial $F_2(x)$ defines the secret sharing of the nonce $k$
such that
\(
k = \sum_{i \in \coalition} d_i + e_i \cdot \bindingf_i
\)
using the associated public data $(m, B)$ to determine $\bindingf_i$.
During the key generation phase,
the first polynomial $F_1(x) = \sum_{j=1}^n f_j(x)$ is generated
such that the secret key shares are $\sk_i = F_1(i)$ and the secret key is $\sk = F_1(0)$.

During the signature phase,
each of the
participants in the coalition $\coalition$ selected for signing use a pair of nonces $(d_i, e_i)$ to
define a degree $\lvert\coalition-1 \rvert$ polynomial $F_2(x)$, interpolating the
values $(i,\frac{d_i + e_i \cdot \HashNon(i, m, B)}{\lambda_i})$,
such that $F_2(0) = \sum_{i \in \coalition} d_i + e_i \cdot \bindingf_i$.

Then let $F_3(x) = F_2(x) + c \cdot F_1(x)$, where
$c = \HashSig(R, \pk, m)$.  Now $z_i$
equals $d_i + (e_i \cdot \bindingf_i) + \lambda_i \cdot \sk_i \cdot c =
\lambda_i (F_2(i) + c \cdot F_1(i)) = \lambda_i F_3(i)$, so $z = \sum_{i \in \coalition} z_i$
is simply the Lagrange interpolation of $F_3(0) = (\sum_{i \in \coalition} d_i + e_{ij} \cdot \bindingf_i) + c
\cdot \sk$.
Because $R = g^{\sum_{i \in \coalition} d_i + e_i \cdot \bindingf_i}$ and $c
= \HashSig(R, \pk, m)$, $(R, z)$ is a
correct Schnorr signature on $m$.
